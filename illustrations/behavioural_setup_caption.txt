A, Subjects were presented with a sequence of either 4 or 5 pure tones, and were asked to imitate by tapping.

B, From the recorded audio signal, tapping onsets were extracted using REPP (Anglada-Tort et al., 2022).

C, To eliminate the effect of participants tapping too fast or too slow, the tempo of the response sequence was normalized to the tempo of the stimulus sequence. This also resulted in a dependent variable we call 'tempo deviation', which was calculated by dividing the total sum of the response ITIs by the total sum of the stimulus IOIs. Then, this value was subtracted from 1, and the absolute value was taken. This last step was taken because we were not interested in whether participants slowed down or speeded up, compared to the stimulus.

D, Using K-means clustering, the complete set of 'symbols' in the participant's responses were organized into k clusters, where the optimal value for k was determined using Silhouette scores. Subsequently, each individual sequence was coded according to these clusters to calculate a participant's G score to quantify systematicity (Jamieson & Mewhort, 2005; Ravignani et al., 2017).

E, Using a Fourier transform, response inter-onset intervals (IOIs) were automatically quantized to the best-describing underlying grid. From the quantized IOIs, Shannon entropy were calculated for the stimulus and the response. 

F, Stimulus-response asynchronies were calculated by summing the absolute differences between each pair of stimulus and response onsets. Tapping variability was calculated as the difference between the coefficient of variation of the tapping ITIs and the coefficient of variation of the stimulus IOIs.

G, From dyads of quantized IOIs within each sequence, interval ratios were calculated, and on- and off-integer ratios were determined following the method in Roeske et al. (2020).
